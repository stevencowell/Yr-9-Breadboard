Bread Board Unit

This unit provides students with the opportunity to gain basic hand skills and confidence in working with timber. Over 10 weeks, students will learn to safely use a range of tools and equipment, master joinery techniques, and document their processes in line with key Industrial Technology outcomes.

Summary & Duration

Duration: 10 Weeks (40-minute lessons, 4 per week)
Focus: Workshop safety, marking & measuring, cutting, joinery, finishing, self-evaluation, and project presentation.

Unit Overview

This “Bread Board” unit (Stage 5) focuses on building practical woodworking skills alongside an understanding of safety and design principles. Students use hand, power, and machine tools to construct a bread board while meeting both theory (5%) and practical (13%) assessment criteria.

Learning Outcomes & Assessment

  • IND51: Manage risks and WHS issues.
  • IND52: Apply design principles to projects.
  • IND53: Demonstrate effective use of tools.
  • IND54: Justify material selection.
  • IND55: Communicate project ideas effectively.
  • IND56: Engage in collaborative work practices.
  • IND57: Transfer skills across projects.
  • IND58: Evaluate project quality on multiple criteria.
  • IND59: Analyse new timber technologies.
  • IND510: Assess the societal and environmental impact of technology.

Assessment: Practical – 13% | Theory – 5%

Week 1: Workshop Safety and Introduction

Introduction

Welcome to Week 1 of the Breadboard unit. This week, your focus is on establishing a safe and productive workshop environment. Safety is paramount in industrial technology, and this article covers the fundamental principles that will guide your practical work.

Understanding WHS Signage

Workplace Health and Safety (WHS) signage is critical in a workshop. These signs use specific colours and shapes to alert you to hazards:

  • Red: Indicates danger or prohibition (e.g. no entry zones).
  • Yellow: Warns of potential hazards such as slippery surfaces.
  • Blue: Provides information and instructions.
  • Green: Denotes the location of safety equipment and emergency exits.

Week 2: Hand Tools and Timber Defects

Introduction

In Week 2, the focus shifts to the effective use of hand tools and the critical process of identifying timber defects. Precision in tool use and recognising imperfections is key to producing high-quality timber work. This in-depth article will guide you through proper techniques, maintenance routines, and best practices to ensure both safety and craftsmanship.

Proper Use of Hand Tools

Hand tools are the cornerstone of traditional woodworking. In this week’s practical sessions, you will work with essential tools such as:

  • Jack Plane: Used for smoothing and shaping timber. Learn the correct angle and pressure required for an even finish. Regular sharpening is essential to maintain its effectiveness.
  • Marking Gauge: Critical for scoring accurate lines along the wood. Ensure that the gauge is calibrated properly before use to guarantee precision in your markings.
  • Try Square: Essential for checking the squareness of workpieces. Using a try square correctly helps ensure that joints are aligned accurately, contributing to the overall quality of your project.
  • Tenon Saw: Designed for precise cutting along marked lines, particularly on the waste side. Controlled, steady strokes are vital for clean cuts and reducing errors.

For further reading on the proper use of hand tools, please visit TeachingWoodwork.com - Hand Tools for Beginners and Young People.

Understanding Timber Defects

Timber is a natural material and often comes with inherent imperfections. Recognising these defects is crucial for selecting the best pieces for your project and ensuring the final product's structural integrity and appearance. Common defects include:

  • Knot: Natural imperfections where branches once grew. While small knots can add character, large or loose knots may weaken the timber.
  • Dent: Impact marks or depressions that can compromise the wood's strength.
  • Irregular Grain: Variations in the wood grain may indicate stress or inconsistency, making it challenging to achieve smooth cuts.
  • Sap Veins: Dark lines that can signal areas of weakness within the timber.

Identifying these defects early allows you to plan your cuts effectively. For example, you may reserve timber with minimal defects for load-bearing components while using less critical pieces for aesthetic features.

Best Practices for Tool Maintenance and Timber Selection

Successful woodworking depends on both well-maintained tools and carefully selected timber. Consider the following practices:

  • Regular Inspection: Always check your tools for damage or dullness and sharpen or repair them as necessary.
  • Timber Selection: Inspect timber thoroughly for defects before starting your project. Mark any imperfections and plan your work accordingly.
  • Organised Workspace: Maintain a clean and organised workspace to minimise accidents and improve efficiency.

Further Research and Discussion

For additional support material on hand tools and timber defects, please visit David's Timber Resource Centre - Wood Defects. This resource offers detailed information on common wood defects, making it accessible and useful for secondary students.

  3. What strategies would you use to work around a timber defect when planning a project?
  4. Discuss the importance of selecting high-quality timber and how recognising defects early can improve the final outcome.

Week 3: Principles of Joinery

Introduction

Joinery is a fundamental aspect of woodworking. In Week 3, you will explore joinery principles with an emphasis on biscuit joinery, which aligns and strengthens timber while enhancing its appearance.

Biscuit Joinery Explained

This method involves inserting oval-shaped biscuits into slots cut in the timber. The biscuits act as alignment aids and reinforce the joint when glue is applied. This method is popular for its simplicity and effectiveness.

The Role of Grain Direction

Proper alignment of the timber grain is critical for joint strength and aesthetics. Well-aligned grain contributes to durability and an attractive finished product.

Safety in Joinery

Follow safety protocols when using tools such as the Biscuit Jointer, Pedestal Drill, and Disc Sander. Always adhere to manufacturer guidelines and wear appropriate PPE.

Further Research and Support Material

For additional support on joinery principles, please watch the following YouTube clip titled How to Use a Biscuit Joiner – Beginner's Guide. This video provides a clear demonstration of using a biscuit joiner safely and effectively, and is appropriate for Year 9 students.

Week 5: Advanced Joining Techniques

Introduction

Advanced joining techniques are the focus of Week 5. This article discusses how to integrate adhesives and clamping methods for strong, precise joints.

Adhesives in Woodworking

Choosing the correct adhesive is crucial. Whether using wood glue or specialised bonding agents, understanding the properties of each adhesive ensures a durable bond between timber pieces.

For further reading on woodworking adhesives, please visit Carbatec - Woodworking Glue Guide.

Clamping and Dry Fitting

Clamping is essential for maintaining proper alignment while the adhesive sets. Dry fitting—assembling the parts without glue first—helps verify the fit and avoid mistakes during the final join.

Safety Considerations

Even when using adhesives, follow safety practices such as proper ventilation and careful handling of clamping tools to prevent accidents.

Week 6: Router Techniques

Introduction

In Week 6, we explore the versatile use of routers in woodworking. Mastering router techniques allows for precision in creating edges, grooves, and intricate designs.

Router Basics

A router is a power tool used to hollow out an area in the face of a piece of wood. It's essential for creating joints, shaping edges, and adding decorative elements.

Types of Router Bits

Understanding the various router bits is crucial:

  • Straight Bits: Used for cutting straight grooves and dados.
  • Rabbeting Bits: Designed for cutting rabbets along the edge of a workpiece.
  • Flush Trim Bits: Ideal for trimming edges flush with a template or another piece of wood.
  • Round-Over Bits: Used to create rounded edges for a smooth finish.

Router Safety

Always prioritize safety when using a router:

  • Wear appropriate personal protective equipment (PPE), including safety glasses and hearing protection.
  • Ensure the router is securely mounted or held firmly to prevent accidents.
  • Keep hands clear of the cutting area and use push sticks or guides when necessary.
  • Always disconnect the power when changing bits or making adjustments.

Advanced Techniques

Once basic skills are mastered, routers can be used for more advanced techniques such as:

  • Dovetail Joints: Creating strong and decorative joints for drawers and boxes.
  • Inlay Work: Adding decorative inlays to enhance the visual appeal of a piece.
  • Template Routing: Using templates to replicate patterns and shapes consistently.
